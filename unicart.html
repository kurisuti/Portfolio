UniCart

UniCart is a browser extension tool that makes shopping a one step checkout process.

View UniCart Prototype

OVERVIEW

UniCart is a browser extension that assists users in their online shopping experiences. UniCart is the ultimate user-friendly shopping companion. All items added from retail stores gets automatically populated into UniCart’s shopping list. Once a user checks out from UniCart, UniCart does the backend work of charging all the vendors.


ROLE & DURATION

UX Designer

Research, Information Architecture,
Interaction, Visual design & testing

Jan 2020 - Mar 2020

Problem

With the e-commerce platform growing, I noticed that most shoppers were transitioning into online shoppers. With how easy and convenient online shopping is, it only takes one click to open multiple browser tabs to different retail stores. However, is there a simpler way for shoppers to check out their carts without having to resubmit the same information over and over again?

Solution

  1. Browser Extension Tool: The tool is non-intrusive and can be opened and closed easily. With each item added to a cart from other retail stores, UniCart also populates the same items to their cart.

  2. One Step Checkout: Goodbye to multiple sign in and checkout processes. Once shoppers sets their payment, shipping, and billing info in UniCart, UniCart uses the information to charge all e-commerce stores all at once.

  3. Wishlist: UniCart tells users the availability of an item. If items are out of stock, users can choose to save the item in their wishlist.

Discovery Phase

When starting this project, I wanted to learn more about my user’s behavior with online shopping and browser extensions. Understanding the user's behavior would help me build a better better understand what features I would need to include in UniCart.

Based on my survey results, 71% of users preferred shopping online versus in-person. During a browsing session, about 65% of users had at least 3-7 tabs opened at a time. When participants were asked how they managed their tabs, 77% of users said they just remove tabs as they browse. The reason users chose to shop online than in person is because online shopping is convenient, websites are available 24/7, and it is an easy checkout process.

Apart from the positives of online-shopping, I also wanted to ask participants about their negative experiences with it. Users complained that the quality of their items were not up to par with what they expected, while others complained about the checkout process and returns. This inspired me to find solutions that could help simplify the checkout process for my users.

The next part of the survey was to talk about browser extensions. On a scale of 1-10, participants were asked how important browser extensions were to them. 47% of users gave browser extensions a 6. In the survey, I wanted to ask participants positive and negative experiences they had with extensions.

“AdBlocker makes my entire online experience more enjoyable...”

For negative experiences, users complained that extensions didn't work as expected.

“...the same/worse discounts that were already offered”.

User Personas

From my user surveys and testing, I was able to create two personas that would be my potential audience.

Mia

Hiring Coordinator | Age: 32 | San Diego, CA

Motivation

Mia doesn't shop much. The only times she does shop is when she sees email/instagram ads with sale deals, or if her family mentions an upcoming deal in their group chat. Mia likes to shop at the comforts of her home, but dislikes how sizes for clothing sites are not consistent. When browsing, Mia likes to delete tabs as she shops. When she revisits her favorite online stores, she wishes that her contact information was saved, so that she can check out quickly.

Frustration

Having to fill out information over and over again, information is hard to read during checkout, sizing and quality of products are not accurate.

"It'd be nice to have a tool that auto-populates all my shopping information."

Oliver

Finance Consultant | Age: 26 | Seattle, WA

Motivation

Oliver loves to window shop online. He doesn't particularly enjoy shopping in-person because there's never things that he really needs. He enjoys shopping at his own pace and own time. Oliver is all about the numbers, so he will only make a purchase when he knows that it is the best deal he can get. Sometimes it takes Oliver a day or two to fully commit to purchasing an item.

Frustration

When products are no longer in stock during checkout, purchasing an item and finding a better price elsewhere.

“On the occasions that I do shop, I want to make sure I'm getting the best deal for my buck."

Competitive Analysis

After having done some user research, I started looking into my market research. The three competitors that I looked into were Honey, Adblock, and CamelCamelCamel.

Honey: Honey is an online coupon extension that works on both desktop and mobile. It helps users find and apply coupon codes when a user is ready to check out. They have a clean simple interface. However, Honey’s mobile application doesn’t provide the same experience that their browser extension does. Therefore, most users don’t use the mobile app.

AdBlock: AdBlock focuses on providing a worry and distraction-free internet experience for their users. Their target audience are mainly concerned with ads that can disrupt their browsing activity. AdBlock is free to download and can work on both desktop and mobile phone. Some of the weaknesses from AdBlock is that they don’t block all advertisements. For a fee, AdBlock will allow ads to be shown. Also some websites deny users access to their website if they have their AdBlock on.

CamelCamelCamel: CamelCamelCamel provides data to users on the price history of products. They provide users information on when is the best time to buy a product. They only track prices and products that are sold on Amazon, but doesn’t clearly state who the third party sites are. It has a poor landing page that may have users to think that their site is not a credible source.

View Wireframe Prototype

With the wireframe prototype, I had three in-person usability tests. Each user had four tasks that they needed to complete.

Task 1: Sign up for an account
Task 2: Creating a new piece of content
Task 3: Organize content
Task 4: Quick description of what each menu icon does

As I observed the partcipants, I saw that each user had no problem signing up for account. They were able to create a new file, however, the ellipsis next to the file name was a little confusing. They simply assumed a drag and drop into the folder would be the action used into adding a file into a folder. All menu tab items were accurately depicted (All Files, Add Documents, Collaborate, Upgrade Account, and Settings), but users were unsure the differences between the Collaboration tab and the All Files tab. One user wanted to see a stronger difference.

High-Fidelity Wireframes

I took the feedback from my usability test and started to mock-up a high-fidelity wireframe of unicart. I added a sidebar to file creation that would show owners of the projects. Users in a shared project would be able to see recent activities done to the project. Collaborative projects also had the commenting section in order to enable better communication skills within teams.

I chose to go with a green as the accent color for unicart. I thought that it was a nice change from the usual blue software color, and that it could be distinguished within the marketplace.

Branding & Identity Phase

The first steps in branding was identifying my target audience, which in this case were remote professionals. Having worked as a freelancer for five years, I understood that one of the key struggles I had was not feeling part of a whole. Oftentimes, details and direction of a project can get lost in translation. All I wanted was more clarity and transparency. I wanted those two words to reflect the values of this product. I liked the word “Clear” used in the product, for it could be interpreted in many ways. For some, it can mean having full transparency, which can eliminate points of cloudiness. I went with the name unicart because it represented the idea of creating your own personal workspace and being able to feel part of a whole.

Logo Design

From the word clear, I was able to branch out to other words that I helped me identify the brand. Some of these words included being transparent, reliable, adaptable, useful, motivational, etc. I used words created from my mind mapping execise to start sketching logo ideas out. I wanted a logo that showed transparency and layers. The logo needed to reflect the name of unicart, as well as, incorporates elements of a workspace in a minimalistic way. The logo would come off as professional, and so I created the logo primarily with geometric shapes.

Color Palette

The next step was establishing the mood board which would come to aid my decision for the color palette. This was personally my favorite part of branding. I searched for images that I thought best represented our product, yet still tied in with the words from the mind map. I drew inspiration from offices that had a lot of natural light, organic natural woods, and plants. I liked the mix of greenery, natural organic material, and industrial spaces all merged together. I used the eye picker to select certain colors that popped out to me. The main accent color that I chose for unicart was a fresh green. I wanted to stay away from blue because it is often a color used in software products. I wanted to make the product visually unique. It also served as a visual challenge for me to work with a color which can be seen as opposite from technology. However, green often reflects growth, and I believe that is what unicart is. It's a platform built to serve a new incoming line of working professionals.

The idea behind the first draft of the high fidelity mock-up was to incorporate stock photo images of work spaces and young working professionals. As stated above in the branding, I used green as an accent color, and incorporated warmer/neutral tones to help balance out the whitespace on the screens. As I added this style throughout the rest of the product, I wanted to do a quick preference testing to see how users felt on some particular screens that I was curious about. I tested out two different color variations, layout, and button placement.

Preference Testing

What color do you prefer? 83% of users chose the second design.

Where do you prefer button placement? 84% of users chose the first design.

What layout do you prefer? 67% of users chose the first design.

From the preference test, there was an overwhelming number of users that prefered the dark blue color theme for the dashboard. This would be a huge change in look from the first high-fidelity mock-up and the second. I also made some changes to some of the onboarding screens that were tested out.

User Testing Phase Part 2

I decided to do a second round of testing in order to add the changes done from my preference testing. The results of this testing would lead to big changes from V2 to my final design look.

A number of users had complained that the “My Files” tab and “Collaboration” tab were too similar in look and functionality to one another.

The only key difference the screens had was the ability to view notes and screen share for collaborative projects. I originally thought collaboration should be a separate tab in itself because it was a highly sought out feature from my user surveys. However, it was such a minor difference of functionality between the two, so I decided to combine the “Collaborate” tab into the “My Files” tab. All files that are shared will be allowed the feature to share notes and screen share.

Settings was difficult to find.

Users didn’t seem to notice that the profile image would have a drop down to access settings. Since I had some extra real estate on my left menu bar, I decided to add a tab that featured “Settings”. This way users had two ways in accessing “Settings”.

Branding of the website was not aligned with the product.

The user said unicart didn’t quite match the look and feel of the website landing page. She wanted to see more clear/layered effects that would match the style of the logo. When this was brought to my attention, I looked into creating a more transparent platform for my users in which they could feel comfortable working in. I created a feature within settings that would allow users to customize their workspace. The side menu bar can be changed based on where ever the remote professional was at.
